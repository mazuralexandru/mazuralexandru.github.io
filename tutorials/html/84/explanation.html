84. Other Inline Semantics: <sub>/<sup> Accessibility & Best Practices

Using <sub> and <sup> correctly is particularly important for accessibility, especially when dealing with mathematical or scientific content.

Accessibility Implications:

  • Screen Reader Annunciation: Many screen readers can announce the presence of subscript or superscript. They might say "subscript" or "baseline lowered" before reading the <sub> content, and "superscript" or "baseline raised" for <sup>.
  • Clarity: This explicit annunciation is often essential for understanding the meaning correctly. Compare hearing "H two O" versus "H subscript two O", or "E equals mc two" versus "E equals mc superscript two" (or "E equals mc squared"). The semantic tags provide the necessary context that visual formatting alone cannot convey to non-visual users.

Best Practices:

  1. Use Semantically: Only use <sub> and <sup> when the text is genuinely subscript or superscript according to established typographical conventions (math, chemistry, ordinals, footnotes).
  2. Avoid for Styling: Do not use <sub> or <sup> simply to make text slightly lower/higher or smaller for purely visual effect. If you need positional adjustments for styling, use CSS properties like position: relative; with top or bottom, or adjust font-size and vertical-align. Misusing these tags adds incorrect semantic meaning and can confuse assistive technologies.
  3. Consider Readability: While necessary for accuracy, excessive use of sub/superscript can sometimes make text harder to read visually. Ensure the context justifies their use. For complex mathematical formulas, consider using MathML (a specialized XML-based markup language for math) for better structure and accessibility, although basic sub/superscript using these HTML tags is often sufficient for simpler cases.

Understand that the <sub> and <sup> tags aren't just changing the appearance; they are adding crucial semantic information that allows screen readers to potentially announce "subscript" or "superscript", enabling correct interpretation of formulas and notations.
