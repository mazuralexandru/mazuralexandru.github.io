76. Citations: Identifying Titles with <cite>

We've used the cite attribute on <blockquote> and <q> to link to a source URL. Now let's look at the <cite> element, which has a different but related purpose: marking up the title of a creative work.

The Citation Element (<cite>)

  • Purpose: Used to identify the title of a creative work. This includes things like:
    • Books (<cite>War and Peace</cite>)
    • Articles (<cite>A Brief History of Time</cite>)
    • Essays, Poems, Songs, Scripts
    • Films (<cite>Citizen Kane</cite>)
    • TV Shows, Games, Operas, Musicals
    • Paintings, Sculptures, Exhibitions
    • It can also be used for legal documents like court cases or statutes.
  • Type: Inline element.
  • Syntax: <cite>...</cite>

Default Rendering:

Browsers typically render the content of a <cite> element in italics.

Important Distinction:

  • Use <cite> ONLY for the title of the work.
  • Do NOT use <cite> for:
    • The name of the author/creator (just plain text, or perhaps specific microdata if needed).
    • The quote itself (use <blockquote> or <q>).
    • A URL (use the cite attribute on <blockquote>/<q>, or an <a> tag for a visible link).

Code Change:

We've restructured the content slightly and added two examples of the <cite> element:

  1. In the paragraph attributing the blockquote (referencing the HTML specification title).
  2. In a new paragraph mentioning a book title.
<body>
  <h1>Quotation Elements & Citations</h1> <!-- Updated H1 -->
  <p>Use <q>inline quotes</q> or <blockquote> for longer ones. To cite the title of a work, use the cite element.</p> <!-- Simplified P, removed cite attribute from q -->

  <blockquote cite="https://www.w3.org/TR/html52/textlevel-semantics.html#the-blockquote-element">
    <p>This is a longer quotation, potentially spanning multiple paragraphs, taken from another source.</p> <!-- Simplified blockquote -->
  </blockquote>
  <p>The source for the above quote can be found in the HTML specification titled <cite>HTML 5.2</cite>.</p> <!-- Added attribution P with <cite> -->

  <p>Another example: I enjoyed reading <cite>The Lord of the Rings</cite> recently.</p> <!-- Added second <cite> example -->

  <!-- <p>This paragraph is temporarily commented out.</p> -->
</body>

(Note: We removed the cite attribute from <q> and simplified the blockquote content for clarity).

✅ Observation:

  1. Locate the two <cite> elements in index.html.
  2. Look at the browser preview. The text "HTML 5.2" and "The Lord of the Rings" should both be rendered in italics, indicating they are titles of works according to the <cite> tag's semantics and default styling.

✨ Key Takeaway: Use the inline <cite> element specifically to mark up the title of a creative work. Do not use it for author names or quotes themselves. It typically renders as italics by default.
