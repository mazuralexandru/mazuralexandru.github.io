81. Preparing for Sub/Superscript: Removing <small> Examples

Having covered the <small> element and its appropriate usage, we'll now remove those examples to prepare for the next set of inline elements: subscript (<sub>) and superscript (<sup>).

Why Remove Code?

  • Focus: To provide a clear context for introducing <sub> and <sup> without distraction from the <small> examples.
  • Relevance: The small print examples are not necessary for understanding subscript and superscript.
  • Clean Slate: Resetting the main content area allows the distinct visual effects of <sub> and <sup> to be easily observed.

Code Change:

We have removed the paragraph containing the first <small> example and the entire <footer> element (which also contained <small>). We've also updated the remaining paragraph text and added a new one.

<body>
  <h1>Other Inline Semantics</h1>
  <p>Now moving on to subscript and superscript elements.</p> <!-- Adjusted P -->

  <!-- Removed paragraph containing <small> -->
  <!-- <p>Here is some text containing <small>small print or side comments</small>, often used for legal notices.</p> -->

  <p>These elements have specific typographical meanings.</p> <!-- Added P -->

  <!-- Removed footer with <small> -->
  <!--
  <footer>
    <p><small>&copy; 2024 LearnHTML Inc. All rights reserved.</small></p>
  </footer>
  -->

  <!-- <p>This paragraph is temporarily commented out.</p> -->
</body>

✅ Observation:

  1. Verify that the elements containing <small> (the paragraph and the footer) are gone from index.html.
  2. The browser preview should show the heading and two plain paragraphs, ready for the introduction of <sub> and <sup>.

✨ Key Takeaway: Removing the <small> examples provides a clean transition to the next topic: subscript and superscript elements.
