77. Citations: <cite> Element Clarifications

Let's reinforce the correct usage of the <cite> element by highlighting what it shouldn't be used for.

Recap: Use <cite> ONLY for Titles of Works

The primary and intended semantic meaning of <cite> in HTML5 is strictly to identify the title of a creative work (book, film, song, article, etc.) or legal/reference document.

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  1. Don't Use <cite> for Author/Person Names:
    • Incorrect: <p>Quote attributed to <cite>John Doe</cite>.</p>
    • Correct: <p>Quote attributed to John Doe.</p> (Use plain text for names unless more specific markup like microdata is being used for other purposes).
  2. Don't Use <cite> for the Quotation Text:
    • Incorrect: <p><cite>To be or not to be, that is the question.</cite></p>
    • Correct: <p><q>To be or not to be, that is the question.</q></p> (Use <q> for inline quotes or <blockquote> for block quotes).
  3. Don't Confuse <cite> Element with cite Attribute:
    • <cite>Title of Work</cite>: An element wrapping the visible title text. Renders as italics by default.
    • <blockquote cite="URL">: An attribute within another tag, holding a URL. Not displayed visually.

Why Precision Matters:

Using <cite> correctly maintains the semantic integrity of your document. It allows assistive technologies, search engines, and potentially other tools to accurately identify references to creative works. Misusing it dilutes its meaning and can lead to incorrect interpretations by machines or confusion for developers reading the code.
