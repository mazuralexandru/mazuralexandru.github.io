44. Inline Semantics: Nesting <em> and <strong>

The <em> and <strong> elements can be nested within each other to indicate increasing levels of emphasis or importance.

Nesting Concept:

  • You can place an <em> tag inside a <strong> tag, or vice-versa.
  • You can also nest multiple instances of the same tag (e.g., <em><em>really</em></em>).
  • The nesting order should follow the standard HTML rule: close the inner tag before closing the outer tag.

Semantic Interpretation:

  • Nesting <em> generally implies a higher degree of stress emphasis.
  • Nesting <strong> implies a higher degree of importance, seriousness, or urgency.
  • Nesting <em> inside <strong> (or vice-versa) combines the meanings – indicating something is both important and requires stress emphasis.

Browser Rendering:

While semantically nesting increases the level of emphasis/importance, browsers often do not have distinct default visual styles for nested levels. For example, <strong>important</strong> and <strong><strong>very important</strong></strong> might both just render as bold text. You would need custom CSS to visually differentiate nested levels if desired.

We've added an example of nesting <em> inside <strong> in the first paragraph of index.html.

✨ Key Takeaway: You can nest <em> and <strong> elements to convey increasing levels of emphasis or importance, or to combine both meanings. Remember the standard rules for proper nesting order.
